# Humanised Research Paper Skill

A complete end-to-end guide for producing research papers that are well-structured, academically
rigorous, and written in a natural human voice — with zero AI-patterned phrasing that would be
flagged by tools like Turnitin, GPTZero, Originality.ai, or similar detectors.

---

## Why AI Detection Triggers (and How to Avoid It)

AI writing has recognisable fingerprints. Understanding them is step one:

- **High perplexity uniformity** — AI tends to use a consistent, mid-range vocabulary. Human
  writers oscillate between simple and complex phrasing unpredictably.
- **Low burstiness** — AI sentences are similarly structured and similar in length. Human writing
  naturally varies: short punchy sentences followed by longer, more complex ones.
- **Transition over-reliance** — AI loves "Furthermore", "Moreover", "Additionally", "It is
  important to note". These are red flags. Minimise or eliminate them.
- **Passive voice overuse** — AI defaults to passive constructions. Human academic writing uses
  passive voice selectively, not habitually.
- **Hedging patterns** — AI uses "It is worth noting that", "This suggests that", "One could
  argue". Human writers hedge differently and less formulaically.
- **Predictable paragraph structure** — AI always opens with a topic sentence, elaborates, then
  concludes. Human writers vary this rhythm.

## Phase 1: Before Writing — Research and Framing

### 1.1 Clarify the Assignment Parameters
Before drafting anything, collect:
- Field / discipline (Sciences, Humanities, Social Sciences, Engineering, etc.)
- Paper type (argumentative, analytical, experimental, literature review, case study)
- Word count and section requirements
- Citation style (APA, MLA, Chicago, IEEE, Vancouver, etc.)
- Target journal, course, or institution (if known)
- Any specific rubric or marking criteria

### 1.2 Develop a Genuine Thesis or Research Question
Do NOT start with a generic statement. The thesis should:
- Take a specific, defensible position or identify a clear gap in existing literature
- Be narrow enough to argue within the word count
- Come from actual reading of sources, not from generating a "plausible" claim

**Bad (AI-typical):** "Climate change is a significant issue that affects many people worldwide."
**Good (human):** "Carbon pricing mechanisms in the EU have been structurally undermined by
sectoral exemptions that disproportionately protect heavy industry, limiting their effectiveness
as decarbonisation tools."

### 1.3 Source Gathering
- Use real academic sources: Google Scholar, PubMed, JSTOR, IEEE Xplore, Scopus, ResearchGate
- Read the actual abstracts and key sections — do not cite sources that haven't been read
- Take rough notes in your own words while reading — this becomes raw material for the paper
- Aim for a mix of foundational/seminal papers and recent publications (within 5 years where
  possible)
- Note direct quotes you may want to use sparingly

## Phase 2: Structure and Outline

### 2.1 Standard Research Paper Structure

**For empirical/scientific papers (IMRaD):**
1. Title
2. Abstract (150–300 words)
3. Introduction
4. Literature Review / Background
5. Methodology
6. Results
7. Discussion
8. Conclusion
9. References

**For argumentative/analytical papers:**
1. Title
2. Abstract (if required)
3. Introduction (with thesis)
4. Background / Context
5. Main Argument Sections (usually 3–5)
6. Counterargument + Rebuttal
7. Conclusion
8. References

### 2.2 Build the Outline Before Drafting
Write one sentence per section describing what it will argue or present. This prevents rambling
and ensures logical flow. Each section should connect causally to the next — not just topically.

## Phase 3: Drafting — Human Writing Techniques

This is the most critical phase. Follow these rules strictly.

### 3.1 Voice and Sentence Rhythm

**Vary sentence length deliberately.**
Short sentences create emphasis. Longer sentences, particularly when they introduce a qualification
or nuance that requires unpacking, are effective for academic elaboration — but they should
not become the default. Alternate between them.

**Use the active voice as the primary mode:**
- "The researchers found" not "It was found by the researchers"
- "This paper argues" not "It will be argued in this paper"

**Vary paragraph openings.** Do not start every paragraph with a declarative topic sentence.
Options include:
- Opening with a concessive clause: "While X is widely accepted..."
- Opening with a question (sparingly, and only when it drives the argument)
- Opening with a specific detail or case before zooming out

### 3.2 Vocabulary and Diction

- Use **domain-specific terminology** naturally — it signals expertise and is less detectable
- Mix formal and moderately informal phrasing where appropriate to the discipline
- Avoid "AI favourite" words: seamlessly, delve, comprehensive, multifaceted, nuanced, pivotal,
  crucial, it's worth noting, in conclusion, in summary, shed light on, robust, landscape (used
  metaphorically), paradigm (overused)
- Use specific, concrete nouns over vague abstractions wherever possible

### 3.3 Argument Construction

Each main section should:
1. Make a specific claim
2. Present evidence (paraphrased or quoted from sources)
3. Analyse the evidence — don't just describe it, interpret it
4. Connect back to the thesis

The **analysis** step is where human writers distinguish themselves. Ask:
- What does this evidence actually prove?
- What does it fail to account for?
- How does it complicate or support the thesis?
- What would a critic say here?

### 3.4 Transition Strategy

Avoid the standard AI transition words. Instead:

| Instead of... | Try... |
|---|---|
| Furthermore | Building on this, / This extends to... |
| Moreover | What compounds this is... |
| Additionally | A related dimension is... |
| It is important to note | Notably, / Crucially, (use rarely) |
| In conclusion | (Just write the conclusion — no label needed in body) |
| This suggests that | The implication here is... / This points to... |

Better still: use **logical connectors that show relationship**, not just sequence:
- Causal: "This explains why...", "As a result..."
- Contrastive: "Yet...", "This stands in contrast to..."
- Concessive: "Even so...", "Despite this..."

### 3.5 Citation and Paraphrasing

**Never use a quote where paraphrase works.** Over-quoting is both an AI pattern and poor
academic practice.

**Good paraphrase:**
- Read the source sentence
- Look away
- Write what it said in your own words, from memory
- Check accuracy, not similarity

**Direct quotes:** Use only when the exact wording is irreplaceable (a definition, a specific
finding, a famous formulation). Integrate them grammatically:
- "As Bourdieu (1984, p. 56) contends, cultural capital functions as..."
- Not: "Bourdieu said: 'cultural capital functions as...'"

**Citation discipline:**
- Cite every claim that is not common knowledge or your own original analysis
- Don't cite claims to seem authoritative — only cite what genuinely supports the point
- Use in-text citations in the correct format for the chosen style

## Phase 4: The Humanisation Pass

After a full draft is complete, run a dedicated humanisation pass. This is a separate editing
stage, not something done while writing.

### 4.1 Flag and Replace AI Patterns

Scan for and rewrite:
- Any sentence starting with "It is", "There are", "This is"
- Any use of: Furthermore, Moreover, Additionally, In conclusion, It is important to note
- Any passive construction that can be made active
- Any sentence longer than 40 words that can be split without losing meaning
- Uniform paragraph lengths — vary them

### 4.2 Add Human Texture

Humans write with:
- **Specific examples** over general claims: "As seen in the 2008 financial crisis" not
  "As history has shown"
- **Mild hedging that sounds natural**: "appears to", "tends to", "in many cases" — not
  formulaic AI hedging
- **Occasional direct address to the reader** (discipline-dependent): "This raises the question
  of whether..."
- **Acknowledgement of limitations** in the argument — AI rarely does this voluntarily

### 4.3 Read Aloud Test

Read the paper aloud from beginning to end. Anywhere that sounds robotic, stiff, or unnatural
when spoken — rewrite it. This is one of the most reliable humanisation techniques available.

### 4.4 Structural Irregularity (Intentional)

Human academic papers are not perfectly uniform. Introduce controlled variation:
- One section can be shorter than the others if the point is made efficiently
- Not every paragraph needs to end with a summary sentence
- Allow one section to open with a brief contextual story or historical moment if it fits the
  discipline and argument

## Phase 5: Abstract Writing

Write the abstract **last**, after the full paper is complete.

A good abstract covers:
1. **Context / Problem** — What is the paper about and why does it matter? (1–2 sentences)
2. **Gap or Question** — What specific problem or gap does this paper address? (1 sentence)
3. **Approach** — How did the paper investigate this? (1–2 sentences)
4. **Key Findings or Argument** — What does the paper conclude or argue? (1–2 sentences)
5. **Significance** — Why does this conclusion matter? (1 sentence)

Abstracts should be written in the simple past (for empirical work) or simple present (for
argumentative work). They should be self-contained — no citations, no abbreviations without
definition, no jargon not used in the paper.

## Phase 6: Final Checklist Before Submission

### Academic Quality
- [ ] Thesis is specific, arguable, and present in the introduction and conclusion
- [ ] All evidence is cited; no uncited claims
- [ ] All sources are real and have been read
- [ ] Counterarguments are acknowledged and addressed
- [ ] Logic flows from section to section without gaps

### Human Voice
- [ ] No AI-typical transition words used (Furthermore, Moreover, Additionally, etc.)
- [ ] Sentence length varies throughout — not uniform
- [ ] Paragraph openings vary — not all topic sentences
- [ ] No passive voice overuse
- [ ] Read-aloud test completed

### Formatting
- [ ] Citation style is consistent throughout (APA / MLA / Chicago / IEEE / etc.)
- [ ] Reference list is complete and correctly formatted
- [ ] Headings, spacing, and font match the required style
- [ ] Word count is within range

## Discipline-Specific Notes

### Sciences / Engineering
- IMRaD structure is mandatory
- Use passive voice more than other disciplines (convention), but still vary it
- Precision over style — exactness matters more than flow
- All data and findings must be cited or from your own study

### Humanities
- Argument and interpretation are the core product
- Allow your own analytical voice to be present — do not hide behind sources
- Close reading of primary texts is expected; secondary sources support, not replace, your reading
- Longer, more complex sentences are acceptable but must remain clear

### Social Sciences
- Balance between quantitative rigour and qualitative interpretation
- Theory sections must link clearly to methodology
- Reflexivity (acknowledging the researcher's position) is often expected

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Listing evidence without analysing it** — Description is not argument
2. **Using too many quotes** — Shows inability to paraphrase; also triggers AI detectors
3. **Vague introductions** — "Throughout history, humans have always..." is a guaranteed red flag
4. **Conclusion that just summarises** — The conclusion should reflect on the significance of
   the argument, not just recap it
5. **Inconsistent citation style** — Mix of APA and MLA in one paper looks careless
6. **Overlong paragraphs** — Academic paragraphs are typically 150–250 words; beyond that,
   split them
7. **Not addressing counterarguments** — A paper without any acknowledgement of opposing views
   lacks credibility and reads as one-sided

## Quick Reference: AI-Flagged Phrases to Avoid

Replace these on sight:

| Flagged Phrase | Human Alternative |
|---|---|
| It is worth noting that | Notably, / [Just state the point directly] |
| Delve into | Examine / Explore / Analyse |
| Shed light on | Clarify / Reveal / Illuminate |
| In today's world / In the modern era | [Name the specific period or context] |
| It goes without saying | [Don't say it — or just say it directly] |
| Crucial / Pivotal / Vital | Important / Key / Central (use sparingly) |
| Comprehensive analysis | [Just do the analysis — don't announce it] |
| This essay will explore | This paper argues / examines / demonstrates |
| In conclusion | [Start the conclusion — no label needed] |
| Multifaceted | Complex / Multi-dimensional / Several-sided |
| Robust | Strong / Reliable / Substantial |
| Landscape (metaphorical) | Field / Area / Context |
| Moving forward | Going forward / In future work / [Rewrite the sentence] |
